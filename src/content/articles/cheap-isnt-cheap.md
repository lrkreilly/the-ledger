The sticker is honest about one thing only: what the object costs on the day you buy it. Every cost after that is invisible at the till — and the cheap version of almost anything carries more of them.

## The cost cadence

Spend more once or spend less several times. That is usually the choice. The cheap version of a pair of boots, a kitchen appliance, a contractor, a piece of software, a roof — all of them push the cost forward into a stream of smaller, more frequent payments. Replacement. Repair. Rework. Time. Frustration. The pretence is that you saved money. The reality is that you rescheduled it.

## Where the cheap version is the right answer

The Ledger is not in the business of telling readers that expensive is always better. Sometimes the cheap version is exactly right:

- The use case is short-lived, low-stakes, or temporary.
- The failure mode is recoverable and the recovery cost is genuinely low.
- The expensive version solves a problem you do not have.
- The premium is paying for a brand, not a quality difference.

The honest question is not "cheap or expensive?" but "which costs am I paying, and over what horizon?"

## When the cheap version is the expensive one

Three signals usually mean the cheap version is the expensive one in disguise:

1. The failure mode is hard to undo. A cheap roof leaks into a ceiling, not just onto a shelf.
2. The replacement schedule is short and the swap is disruptive. The cost is not just the object — it is the day you lose installing the next one.
3. Your time has a real opportunity cost. The hours spent re-fixing, re-buying, re-hiring are hours not doing something else.

Cheap is not cheap. It is a payment plan with hidden instalments. Sometimes the plan is worth it. Often it isn't. The work is in seeing the instalments before you sign for them.
