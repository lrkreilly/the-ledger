A quote is not a price. It is a price for a defined scope. The two quotes you are comparing are not pricing the same thing — and the cheap one is the one most aggressive about what it has decided not to include.

## What the cheap quote is optimising for

Quotes get cheap by removing things. The removals are usually invisible to the person reading the quote and obvious to the person who wrote it. Common ones:

- **Scope reduction.** Smaller area, fewer fittings, fewer prep steps, fewer rooms.
- **Material substitution.** Cheaper paint, thinner timber, lower-grade fixings, generic parts.
- **Process compression.** One coat instead of two. Skipping primer. Not protecting adjacent surfaces.
- **Risk transfer.** Day-rate variations for anything unexpected. "Discoveries" billed extra.
- **Warranty exclusions.** "Workmanship only" — not the parts, not the surfaces underneath.
- **Their own margin.** A bid priced to win, not to deliver well.

Most of these are not dishonest. They are choices about what to do for the money. The cheap quote is optimised. The question the reader has to answer is whether it is optimised for the things they care about.

## The comparison that actually works

Comparing totals doesn't work. Comparing scopes does. A useful read of two quotes asks:

1. What is each quote actually doing? List the included tasks, not the line items.
2. Where do they differ? The differences are the real price.
3. What is each quote not doing? The exclusions and the assumptions are where surprises live.
4. What variation rules apply? A cheap base price with open-ended variation language is a different product to a higher fixed price.

When the cheap quote and the expensive quote are doing the same job at the same standard with the same risk allocation, you have a price difference. Until then, you have a scope difference.

## The honest conclusion

Sometimes the cheapest quote is the right one. The job is small. The risks are low. The contractor is good and bidding to fill a quiet week. That happens. It is not the default. The default is that price difference reflects scope difference — and the work is to find out what was dropped to land the number.
