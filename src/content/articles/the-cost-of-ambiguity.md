There is no such thing as "we'll figure it out as we go." There is only "someone will figure it out, alone, in the moment, with whatever information they happen to have." Ambiguity is not the absence of a decision. It is a decision to delegate the decision to whoever turns up.

## Where ambiguity gets paid for

Ambiguity has predictable bills:

- **Rework.** The job is done. Then it is done again, differently, because "done" was never defined.
- **Variance.** Two people do the same task and produce two different outputs. Both think they did it right.
- **Escalation.** Decisions that should take minutes take hours, because there is no standard to point at.
- **Disputes.** "That wasn't what I meant." "That wasn't what I was told." Both are true.
- **Quiet attrition.** The good people get tired of being the standard. They leave.

None of these costs show up on a budget line. They show up in the time it takes to finish things, in the meetings that exist to clean up, and in the work that has to be redone the week after it shipped.

## What a standard actually does

A standard does not constrain good work. It absorbs the cost of defining "good" so that the work in front of someone is the job, not the meta-job of inventing the rules. Standards make the obvious easy and the unusual visible.

A useful standard is not a long document. It is a short description of:

1. What the output looks like when it is right.
2. What the output looks like when it is wrong.
3. What to do when the situation does not match either.

That is most of it. Standards fail when they try to be exhaustive, and they fail again when they are absent. The work is in the middle.

## When ambiguity is the right answer

Sometimes the standard genuinely does not exist yet. The work is new, the situation is unusual, the right answer is "let's figure it out." Fine. But that should be a stated condition, not a default. The cost of ambiguity comes from pretending it isn't there.
