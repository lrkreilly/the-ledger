> *None of those constraints make it dishonest. All of them make it partial.*

A school report sums up the parts of learning that are easy to measure, measured in one setting by one person, based on what that person decided to look for. These limits don't make the report dishonest, but they do make it incomplete.

Most parents read a report in one of two ways. They skim to see if the grades and comments sound broadly fine, and move on. Or they fix on a single low mark, unfamiliar phrase, decide something is wrong, and escalate. Both responses lean on the report to settle a question that the report cannot fully settle.

What a report can do, it does within its scope. What it cannot do is often the part that most parents do not think to read for. The work is to separate the two, format by format and decision by decision, so the page is read for what it actually says.

## What a report is actually measuring

Three things sit between what the school does and what a parent reads on the page.

The first is the underlying thing the school is trying to teach: reading comprehension, mathematical reasoning, the ability to plan and finish a piece of written work, and how the child handles uncertainty when faced with a problem they have not seen before. These are the goals. They are mostly not measurable in any direct way.

The second is the assessment instrument: a test, a project, an in-class observation, or a marking rubric that the teacher applies. The instrument is a proxy. It is designed to surface evidence of the underlying thing in a form that a teacher can mark. It does this well in some subjects and partially in others, and the gap between the underlying thing and the instrument is rarely zero.

Third is the report format: letter grades, percentages, short comments, effort marks, or colour codes. This format compresses the teacher's work into something a parent can read in five minutes.

A report is the end of that chain. The compression is the value: a parent does not need every marked task or every note to make sense of where the child is. The compression is also the limit. By the time it reaches the page, the report is several steps removed from what it claims to describe.

This is not a fault of reports. It is what reports are for. The point of naming the three layers is to help the reader know which layer they are looking at when they read a grade. A/B+ is information about the third layer. It is partial information about the second. It is a translation of partial information about the first.

## What the numbers actually mean

Different report formats compress in different ways. Reading a report well starts with knowing which kind of compression you are looking at.

*Letter grades and percentages.* A/B+ or 78% is the average of several marked tasks, weighted by the teacher according to a scheme that is not usually shown to parents. Two students with the same final grade can have very different patterns: one consistent across all tasks, one who scored high on the assessments that counted most and low on the ones that counted less. The grade hides the pattern. If the report breaks the grade down into components, read the components before the headline. If it does not, the grade is best treated as a rough band rather than a precise score.

*Standardised test scores.* Three numbers commonly appear and are commonly confused. A raw score is the number of items answered correctly. A scaled score adjusts the raw score for the difficulty of the particular test form, so two students who sat different versions can be compared. A percentile rank indicates where the student sits relative to a comparison group: a 70th percentile means the student scored above 70% of the comparison cohort, not that they answered 70% of the questions correctly. Percentiles compress more than parents usually realise. The difference between the 70th and 80th percentiles can be a handful of items on a 30-item test. Treat small percentile movements between sittings cautiously. The measurement error on a single test sitting is large enough that a few percentile points either way can be the test, not the child.

*Meeting expectations and rubric-based reporting.* A phrase such as *meeting expectations* or *working at standard* is a judgement relative to a benchmark someone has set. Two questions matter for reading it: who set the benchmark, and against what cohort. A child meeting expectations in one school may not be meeting expectations in another, because the schools have set the benchmark differently. A child meeting expectations against the year group is not the same as a child meeting expectations against a national or international standard. The phrase says little on its own. What it is referenced to is everything.

*Effort and achievement marks.* Where a report carries both, the effort mark can be one of the most useful lines on the page. Read it as the teacher's observation of how the child has engaged from week to week, not as a separate measurement. Achievement is driven by more than the grade implies: how the task was designed, how the rubric was applied, and where the child started the year. The effort mark, read more as observation rather than measurement, can carry information that the achievement number does not. It is also the line most parents skim past because it carries no obvious decision. A child who achieves well with low effort and a child who achieves moderately with high effort are in very different positions, and only one of those positions is something the parent can build on.

## What doesn't fit on the page

A report has room for what fits a report. Most of what a teacher knows about a child does not fit. Not because anything is hidden, but because a one-page format with grades and a paragraph of comments can carry only so much.

What gets left out tends to cluster around behaviours and personality that are real, observable, and often more relevant to decisions than the academic marks.

How the child handles being wrong: do they sit with it and try again, or do they shut down. Do they ask questions, and if so, what kind. How they respond to unstructured time: do they read, draw, find someone to talk to, or do they stall? Who they tend to sit with and how that group is doing. Whether they volunteer or only speak when asked. How they look at the end of the day compared with the start. What they bring up unprompted and what they avoid.

None of this is in the report. Most of it is in the teacher's head.

Surfacing it is a matter of asking questions that the teacher can answer specifically. Open questions reveal open answers. *How is she doing* invites *she is doing well*, which says little. More specific questions work better.

*What does she do when she gets something wrong?* The answer separates a child who is learning from a child who has learned to look like they are learning.

*Who does she sit with at lunch?* The answer is a read on belonging, which predicts more of a child's school experience than the academic marks do.

*What does she ask about that I would not expect?* The answer surfaces interests and uncertainties that a curriculum-shaped report cannot capture.

*What is one thing she has improved on this term that you didn't predict in week one?* The answer tests whether the teacher has actually been watching, and gives the parent something concrete that the report did not.

These questions are not interrogations. They are requests for the part of the teacher's read that the format does not have room to print.

## How to use it

A single report is a data point. A pattern across three or four reports is a signal. Most decisions worth making from school reports want a pattern, not a moment.

This matters because a single low mark, a single dip, or a single sharp comment can pull a parent into responding with data that the data does not yet support. The child had a hard week. The teacher marked harder this term. The format changed. The assessment instrument behaved differently for this cohort. Each of these is more common than parents assume, and each can move a grade or a phrase enough to register as a change.

A few rules of thumb for separating signal from noise.

A grade movement of one band within a subject, with no other supporting signal, is usually within normal variation. A movement of two bands, or a one-band movement that lines up with a comment, an observation at home, or a change in the child's mood about the subject, is worth attention.

A negative pattern across several subjects in the same term, especially one that lines up with something the parent has observed, is worth attention regardless of the size of any single change.

A positive pattern that is sharper than the parent's own read of the child should also be questioned. Reports that look stronger than what the parent sees at home are not necessarily wrong, but are worth understanding before they are accepted.

A report that contradicts what the parent observes is not automatically the wrong half of the contradiction. The parent's read carries weight because the parent sees more hours of the child. The report carries weight because the teacher sees the child in a setting the parent does not. The honest move when the two disagree is to gather more from both sides before deciding which to trust, not to assume one or the other.

Not every report needs action. Most do not. A pattern worth attention is not necessarily a pattern worth a new intervention; sometimes the right move is to note it and let the next reporting period add to the picture.

When a report does warrant action, the order of conversations matters. The first conversation is with the child, framed as curiosity rather than confrontation: what is going on in this subject, what feels hard, what feels easy. The second conversation is with the teacher, framed as wanting their read rather than wanting an answer: what they see, what they would suggest, what they would watch for. The third conversation is with the school, if the first two have not resolved what the parent is trying to understand.

## When the report and what you see don't match

Two situations need calling out because they are common and they go in opposite directions.

*When the report looks fine but the child is struggling.* The grades are within band, the comments are warm, and the parent is left wondering why the child is anxious, avoidant, tired, or quietly unhappy about school. Reports can look fine for several real reasons that do not mean the school is hiding anything. A child can be coping by working harder than is sustainable, hitting the grades through effort the report cannot see. A child can be in a subject area where the assessment has not yet caught up to the difficulty the child is experiencing. A child can be socially uncomfortable in a way that does not affect the marks. A child can be on the easy side of a class where the academic measurement is satisfied long before the engagement is. The report is not wrong in these cases. It is doing what it can do. What it cannot do is read the parts of the child's school experience that do not show up on a marked piece of work.

*When the report looks concerning but the child seems fine.* A single low grade, a sharp comment, or a sudden change in band can pull a parent into worry that the situation does not support. The most common reasons are simple ones: the child had a hard week, the assessment was harder for this cohort, the teacher's standards shifted, the format of the test penalised a way of working that is a real strength elsewhere. A child who reads well but freezes in a timed comprehension test can produce a low score that says more about timed tests than about reading. A child who answers correctly through a method the teacher did not teach can be marked down for the method even though the answer is right. The report in these cases is also not wrong. It is recording what the instrument measured. The instrument did not measure what the parent thought it was measuring.

The general move in both directions is the same. Read the report alongside what is already known about the child, ask what the report measured and how, and treat any contradiction as a prompt to look closer rather than a verdict to act on.

## What to do with one

A school report is a partial picture, captured by imperfect instruments, summarised into a form a parent can read in a few minutes. That summary is useful. It is also not the answer to the question most parents are actually asking when they open it, which is some version of *is the child learning, and is the child all right.*

Read it for what it shows. Ask about what it does not. Take it seriously, take it lightly, and do not confuse it with the question of whether the learning is real.
