A school report is a summary of the parts of learning that are easy to measure, observed in one setting, by one observer, against a brief that observer chose. None of those constraints make it dishonest. All of them make it partial.

## What a report can tell you

A school report carries real information. It says what the student produced when asked to produce it, where that sits against a class or year-group cohort, and how the teacher reads progress and behaviour over a window. For decisions about whether a child is reaching the standards the school has set, in the format the school uses to assess them, the report is a fair signal.

It also tells you something about the teacher's read of the room — which is its own useful signal, even if it isn't the one the report claims to be giving.

## What a report cannot tell you

A report cannot tell you whether the student understood the material or learned to perform on the test. Both produce the same grade. It cannot tell you whether the curriculum is well-designed, whether the same student would flourish under a different brief, or what they would be capable of with different support. It cannot tell you what the student will remember six months later. It cannot separate the student's ability from the conditions they were assessed under: noise, sleep, mood, the day's relationship with the teacher.

The most important parts of learning — durability, transfer to new problems, motivation that survives the end of term — are usually not in the report because they are not what a report can measure.

## What to do with one

Use the report for what it actually is: one observer's structured assessment of one slice of the work, in one setting, against one brief. It is more useful than no report. It is less useful than the report's authority suggests. The honest reading is to take it seriously, take it lightly, and not confuse it with the question of whether the learning is real.
