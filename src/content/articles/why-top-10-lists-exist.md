> *The useful question about a "top 10" list is not which entry to choose. It is who needed the list to exist, and what they needed it to do.*

A cleaning company in Auckland publishes a page on its own site titled *Top 10 Cleaning Companies in New Zealand*. At number one: the company itself. The page is laid out as a ranking. It uses the format of objective comparison. It carries the visual signal of editorial work. None of that is true. The page is an advertisement, wearing the shape of journalism.

This is the most obvious version of the genre, and the easiest to see through once it is named. The harder versions are the ones where the commercial relationship is one step removed. The affiliate site that ranks ten products and earns a commission on every click. The SEO page that copied its ranking from three other SEO pages. The industry-adjacent blog whose "top 10 painters in Wellington" list happens to be ten painters from a single referral network. None of these pages explain what they are. Most are read by people who think they are reading research.

## The shape is the point

Most "top 10" lists are not the work of someone who tested ten things and ranked them. They are the work of someone who needed traffic, payment, or attention, and built a page in the shape of a ranking because that shape captures clicks.

The shape does the work the content does not. *Top 10* promises the reader has been saved hours of research. The format implies someone competent has done the comparison. The numbers imply judgment: that the second entry was carefully measured against the first, by criteria the reader can rely on.

In the majority of lists encountered through search results, none of this is true. No one tested anything. No one compared anything. The ranking is the page, and the page is the product. The reader is the unit being sold.

## What a real ranking looks like

Some "top 10" lists are real. Consumer publications without commercial stake in what they review, peer-reviewed academic rankings, government licensing bodies that publish provider lists with disclosed criteria, journalism from outlets whose business is publishing. These exist. They are also rare on the open web, particularly in service categories (cleaning, plumbing, building, tutoring, dentistry, law) where independent journalism is thin and search results are dominated by commercial pages.

A real ranking can be recognised by what it does and what it is willing to do. It discloses its methodology. It discloses its funding. It allows disagreement. It is willing to rank things its publisher does not own, and to refuse to rank things its publisher does. It updates when the underlying situation changes. It is published by an organisation whose primary business is publishing, not the thing being ranked.

A list that does all of this is worth reading. The vast majority of lists do none of it.

## What top 10 lists are optimised for

Most lists fall into one of six commercial shapes. Knowing which shape a list belongs to is the difference between using it and being used by it.

*Self-ranking.* The business itself publishes a list of its category and places itself first. Optimised for capturing search traffic from queries like *best [trade] in [city]* and converting that traffic into its own sales. The other nine entries are filler. Some are real competitors, named to make the page look credible. Some are companies that have no idea they are on the list. The page also cheapens the brand it was meant to promote. A reader who looks twice sees a business that would rather grade its own work than be reviewed on it.

*Affiliate revenue.* The publisher earns a commission for every click or purchase made through the page. Optimised for sending the reader to the option that pays the highest commission, not the option that is best. Sometimes disclosed in small print at the bottom of the page. Often not.

*SEO traffic.* The page exists to rank on Google for high-volume search terms like *best [thing] 2026*. Optimised for ranking, not for accuracy. The actual content is often copied from earlier SEO pages, generated by AI, or assigned without method. The page collects ad revenue per view, regardless of whether the reader makes a decision.

*Industry placement.* A trade association, supplier network, or referral group publishes a list whose members are also paying participants in some way. Optimised for directing customers to those participants. The list looks like a neutral overview of the field. It is the field, restricted to people who have paid to be on it.

*Pay-to-play.* Businesses pay an explicit fee for the ranking position. Sometimes labelled as *sponsored* or *featured*. Sometimes labelled as nothing. The order of the list is the order of the fee schedule.

*Programmatic content.* Generated by AI or content mills with minimal human review. Optimised for producing many ranking-shaped pages across many search terms, at near-zero cost. No one has tested anything. No one has read the list before it was published.

A seventh shape exists. The genuine ranking, made by people who tested or evaluated what they ranked, disclosed their method, and are willing to be wrong publicly. It is rare on the open web. Where it appears, it is usually published by an organisation whose business model does not depend on the outcome of the ranking.

## How to tell what you are reading

The diagnostic questions for a top 10 list are short and reveal more than most readers expect.

*Who publishes the list, and what is their business?* The highest-leverage question on the page. A cleaning company publishing a list of cleaning companies is in the cleaning business, not the journalism business. The list is marketing whether or not it says so.

*Does the list disclose how it was assembled?* A real ranking tells the reader what was tested, by whom, against what criteria. A fake ranking tells the reader nothing because there is no method to disclose. Methodology language that says only *we evaluated each company based on quality, reliability, and customer satisfaction* is the absence of methodology, dressed in its vocabulary.

*Does the list disclose its financial relationships?* Affiliate links, paid placements, sponsorships, ownership of any business on the list. The honest list says so at the top. The dishonest list either hides the disclosure or buries it under the fold.

*Are the descriptions substantive or interchangeable?* Read the entries themselves. A real ranking has specific reasons for each placement. A fake ranking has marketing copy in which every business is *trusted*, *reliable*, *professional*, or *the best in the region*. If the descriptions could be swapped between entries without anyone noticing, the ranking is not a ranking.

*Could the list have placed someone the publisher dislikes at the top?* A real ranking is willing to. A list whose publisher cannot conceive of putting a competitor first is not a list.

*Does anyone outside the page refer to it?* Real rankings are cited by other publications, regulators, journalists, or industry bodies. A list that exists only on the page where it was published, with no external uptake, is doing work that does not require external recognition. It is doing search-traffic work.

## What the reader is actually deciding

When a reader reaches a top 10 list, they are usually at the start of a real decision. They are looking for help. They have time pressure, limited information, and a reasonable hope that someone has done the comparison work they cannot do themselves.

The list is built to look like that help. The shape of the page does the work that no actual research did. By the time the reader has clicked through to a recommended provider, they have been moved through a marketing funnel they did not know was a funnel.

The harm is not small. The same dynamic plays out across decisions that matter: choosing a tradesperson, a school, a tutor, a healthcare provider, a lawyer, a financial planner. Each of these categories has its own top 10 lists, optimised for the same six commercial shapes. Each is read by people who think they are reading the result of research.

The decision in front of the reader is not which of the ten options to choose. The decision is whether to trust the page itself. Most of the time, the answer is no. Not because the publisher is necessarily malicious, but because the structural incentives that produced the page did not include telling the reader the truth.

The work top 10 lists pretend to do is hard. It takes hours per item. It requires the publisher to recommend things that do not pay them, and to refuse to recommend things that do. Most of the web is not built that way, because most of the web is monetised through traffic and commission. A list that did the work would look different, would cost the publisher more to produce, and would attract less of the traffic the rest of the web is competing for.

Read who published the list. Read what they sell. Read whether any of it is disclosed. Most lists do not survive those three questions. The list claims to do the work for the reader. Usually the work has been done on the reader instead.
