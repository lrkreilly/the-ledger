A "Top 10" list is not an opinion. It is a format — and the format makes money, ranks in search, and gives a tired reader a feeling of finality. The list of items is the bait. The format is the product.

## What the format is optimised for

Three structural incentives keep "Top 10" content alive:

- **Search.** "Best X 2026" is one of the highest-intent queries on the internet. Lists rank for it because they look like answers. Search engines reward content that looks decisive.
- **Affiliate revenue.** Every ranked item is a buying recommendation, and most ranked items link out through an affiliate programme. The list earns money per click, sometimes per purchase. The rank is, in part, a payment structure.
- **Reader closure.** A list ends. It says "these are the answers." Readers reward content that feels finished. The format performs the act of having decided so the reader does not have to.

None of these incentives reward being right. They reward being ranked.

## What the format cannot do

A "Top 10" list cannot answer the only question that matters: which of these works for *you*. The list ranks against an averaged buyer, an averaged use case, an averaged budget. None of those people exist. The reader who reads the list is specific. The list is not.

The honest version of the work — "here are the trade-offs, here is when each option is good enough, here is what you give up" — does not rank as well in search, does not convert as well in affiliates, and does not feel as decisive to a tired reader. So the honest version is rarer.

## The Ledger's posture

The Ledger does not publish "Top 10" lists. Not because ranking is dishonest in principle — sometimes a genuine ranking with declared methodology is useful — but because the format is heavily optimised against the reader and the editorial pull toward producing them is constant. Sitting it out is easier than performing it well.
